Preparing for an Earthquake and the Threat of Predatory Finance

From the first settlers and for more than a hundred years,
people building the neighborhoods of Portland
didn’t know they were in earthquake country.
And now,
brick chimneys
are a critical vulnerability for many homeowners.
This could be an opportunity for a small business.
And maybe the Portland Clean Energy Fund can be used
to retrofit chimneys in low-income households,
or to employ minority-owned businesses
to reinforce every tall, narrow and hollow stack of unreinforced masonry
in all the neighborhood homes. As a collective action, retrofitting brick chimneys
throughout the city
could help us all
to consider how the shape of urban life
might be restructured after the earthquake.


As we build resilience into our city,
we can begin the transition to a low-carbon economy
and since prior generations of urban planning and sunk investments
prevent significant changes to the location of freeways and railroads,
maybe we could ask what big changes
to transportation, housing, and lifestyles,
can happen after a massive earthquake,
when so much of the city will be rebuilt?
Can we begin to imagine a better city?


Portland needs everyone to realize its greatest potential.
We can use participatory political processes to plan a city that will benefit all residents.
People can improve upon the past.
If we don’t plan for the city we want to live in,
after people put out the fires,
the only plan they have will be their memory of how Portland used to be
and we have the photos, the city has been entirely documented—
in our grief, in our need to get back to work, in honor of what was lost,
what if the city is rapidly rebuilt exactly as it was?
Modern construction methods and machines allow us to build an entire city, fast.
Will the time after the quake be one of realizing a collective vision?
Or maybe the disaster will be used to concentrate power and wealth by a few big players.



Interdependent lifelines and coming threats


Liquid fuel will be needed to power emergency vehicles and bulldozers,
but our storage tanks are along the Willamette north of downtown, upon unstable ground.
There’s a pipeline from Seattle but that’s not seismically resilient either.
To clear the roads we need heavy machinery,
but to transport fuel we need to repair pipelines and roads.
All our critical lifelines are interdependent.
And even if every resident has two weeks supply of emergency water,
that won’t be enough.
All the waterlines broken by a megathrust along the Cascade corridor
won’t be repaired in two weeks time, much less the electric grid
to power the pumping stations.
It could be a year without water and sewer in Portland.


The Cascadia subduction zone earthquake will divide time between before and after:
the post-earthquake city. When lifelines and routines have been disrupted for months—
what if you didn’t work the job you currently have for six months or for as long as a year,
if your employer or your own business is a casualty
of the damage from the earthquake, fires, and economic disruption—
people without savings could lose their homes. And if people can’t pay the rent,
that property owner might default. If you work remote,
when you get back online, what about your water?
When an earthquake breaks apart roads, waterlines, power and sewage,
with the downtown cordoned off,
a no-go zone surrounding one-and-a-half times the height of buildings,
the ability to make money suddenly shifts,
combined with necessary repairs, say a brick chimney crashed through the roof,
and the expense of making the mortgage may force many to walk away.


Earthquake codes only require the building to be survivable.
If you are building beyond the minimum requirements, making structures
that can still be used after a 9.0 subduction zone earthquake,
you’re building for our region. You are building for a unique geology and climate.
Resilience is a banner word for an environment deformed by global warming,
when we have come to expect disruption. Building resilience is not for a niche market,
it’s more than awesome marketing. Resilience in the built environment embodies
our shared cultural awareness of multiple combined threats, tipping points,
and cascading collapse. The loss of biodiversity, resource depletion,
population pressure, and climate-driven migration. You know, in the years ahead,
the water-rich region of the Pacific Northwest looks pretty good.



Speculative finance and imagined futures


In a disaster, people move fast. Money moves faster. Asset managers and algorithms
buying and selling at the right moment. Sometime in the future, a warmer, drier planet
makes this region even more desirable than it already is. It’s no secret.
The money to buy homes, this land, will be available to a networked world of investors.
These properties could be targeted by global finance. Water-rich regions of the world
are already targets. And our farms, with their access to market on roadways disabled,
could farmland of the Willamette Valley be vulnerable too?


Some people will leave by choice.
When our toilets no longer flush,
when trucks can’t deliver to our doorsteps,
when everyone who came here for the doughnuts has long gone,
the people who remain will live upon the land. They will remain for the land.
For the love of this land. This place on earth. People will live here
as they have lived in this place for thousands of years before us.
They will repair and rebuild.
If we are prepared, we will improve the city and have passed legislation
to prevent predatory finance from capturing land in the Pacific Northwest,
to prevent the financialization of our cities and way of life.


Can we, as a people,
our representatives, the state,
can we protect ourselves from disaster capitalism
in a time of vulnerability? What is more powerful, global finance or the state?


Now it is only speculation,
a novel about financial technology, You Will Win The Future
follows an eccentric group of people, each embody different ideas, powerful ideas
about themselves and their world that can so fully capture one, and these ideas are fixated
upon our world, the Pacific Northwest, Portland’s future. The story is set in the near-future,
the trends carrying forward our consensual reality.
An exploration, with science as its foundation, this book is an exploration
of an unknown future in which we have only the past, our present.
A massive amount of data we can project along trendlines into the future.
